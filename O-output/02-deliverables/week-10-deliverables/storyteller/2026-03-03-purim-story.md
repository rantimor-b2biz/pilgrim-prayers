## Opening: The Moment

A girl in hiding. A king who doesn't know her name. A rival named Haman writing a decree across parchment: on a single day, all the Jews will be destroyed.

That's how the Book of Esther begins. And if you read it without the distance of 2,500 years, it doesn't feel ancient. It feels like it could be tomorrow.

---

## The Crisis

Haman is confident. The decree is signed. The machinery of empire is set in motion. The people are in despair.

But in that moment, in the place where no one was paying attention, something happens. Esther speaks. Mordecai calls for a fast. Prayer moves in the quiet places.

The reversal is complete. Haman is undone. The people Haman meant to destroy are the ones left standing.

---

## The Celebration

Purim doesn't commemorate victory in battle. It commemorates *survival against the odds*. And so the festival itself is joyful. Defiant, even. There are feasts, costumes, wine, and celebration.

Because the point isn't to live in fear of Haman. The point is to remember that Haman lost.

---

## The Geography

Here's what matters: this story takes place in Persia. The very land that, 2,500 years later, believers from around the world long to visit.

They want to stand in Jerusalem. They want to place their prayers at the Western Wall. They want to be, even for a moment, in the land where this ancient story happened. In the land where faith survived an empire.

---

## Why It Resonates Now

The world feels divided. Politics feel overwhelming. Empire feels powerful. And then there's this 2,500-year-old story that says: empires come and go. But prayers reach deeper than borders.

Believers know this in their bones. They feel it when they think about Jerusalem. They remember it when they pray.

And that's why, this week, prayers are being placed at the Western Wall by people who can't be there in body but are spiritually present in spirit. They're continuing the same story. They're saying the same thing Esther said: I am here. My prayer matters. I will not disappear.

---

## The Closing: What Purim Teaches

Purim teaches that:
- You don't have to be in the exact place to be spiritually present there
- Faith survives what politics cannot
- The smallest act of courage (a girl speaking to a king) can reverse the machinery of empire
- Joy and celebration are not frivolous — they're how you remember you survived
- Prayers reach farther than anyone knows
