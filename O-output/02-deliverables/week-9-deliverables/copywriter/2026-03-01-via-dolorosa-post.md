## The Road That Still Receives

The Via Dolorosa does not announce itself.

You turn a corner in the Old City and you are already on it: a narrow stone street, rising slightly, lined with merchants selling spices and postcards and olive wood crosses. The everyday and the sacred pressed so close together you cannot find the line between them.

In March, in the early morning, before the merchants open, the street is different.

The cold of the night still lives in the stone. The air smells like rain on old rock. A small group of pilgrims moves slowly upward, led by a Franciscan friar carrying a worn wooden cross. Their feet make small sounds on the wet stone. No one speaks.

At the Fourth Station, a woman stops. She wears a gray wool coat. She presses one hand flat against the wall, closes her eyes, and stays that way until the group has moved on without her.

Whatever she is carrying, she has brought it here.

The Via Dolorosa receives what you bring.

---

This is the road Jesus walked to Golgotha. Carrying the cross. Through a city that did not stop what it was doing, through heat and noise and the pressing weight of something that was not supposed to be possible for a human body to carry.

The stones beneath the pilgrim woman's feet are the same stones.

Isaiah called him "a man of suffering, and familiar with pain."

Familiar. Not distant from it. Not observing it from somewhere clean and safe. Familiar with it. The way you are familiar with something that has lived close to you for a long time.

That is the thing that pulls people to this street.

Not to see where Jesus died. But to bring what they are carrying to the one person in history who already knows the weight of it.

---

Carol, from Tennessee, could not come to Jerusalem.

She is her husband's caregiver. He has cancer. She has not slept a full night in months. This Lent, her pastor asked the congregation where they would bring their heaviest burden, if they could go anywhere.

She knew before he finished the question.

She wrote three words on a small piece of paper: "Carry him, Lord."

She folded it. She mailed it. A real person placed it on the Via Dolorosa, at one of the stations where the road rises and the weight does not get lighter.

Prayers travel farther than feet.

---

If you are carrying something this Lent, this road has held heavier. And it still receives.
