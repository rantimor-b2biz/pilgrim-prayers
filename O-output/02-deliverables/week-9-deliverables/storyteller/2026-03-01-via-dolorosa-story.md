# Storyteller Draft: Via Dolorosa
**Week 9 | Date:** 2026-03-01
**Agent:** Storyteller
**Status:** Draft - ready for Copywriter

## The Story Foundation

**Setting:** Via Dolorosa, Jerusalem. A cold Tuesday morning in early March.
**Character:** A woman named Carol, 64, from Tennessee. She has been her husband's caregiver for two years. He has cancer. She has not slept a full night in months.
**Her prayer:** Three words. "Carry him, Lord."

---

## The Narrative Core

Carol has never been to Jerusalem. She never thought she would need to go - her faith was always the same distance as her living room, her kitchen table, the pew she has sat in for thirty years. But this Lent, something shifted.

Her pastor asked the congregation: where would you take your heaviest burden if you could go anywhere in the world?

She knew before he finished the question.

The road Jesus walked.

Not a church. Not an altar. The actual road. The one where every stone has witnessed someone carrying something impossible, where the path rises and the weight does not get lighter, where a man named Simon was pulled from the crowd to help carry what no one should carry alone.

She cannot go. She knows that. But she also knows that the road is still there.

And prayers travel farther than feet.

---

## The Narrative: Scene on the Via Dolorosa

The Via Dolorosa does not announce itself.

There is no grand gate, no silence zone. You turn a corner in the Old City and you are on it: a narrow stone street, slightly rising, lined with merchants selling everything from spices to postcards to olive wood crosses. The everyday and the sacred pressed together so tightly they cannot be separated.

In March, in the early morning, the street is different.

The merchants are not yet open. The cold of the night is still held in the stone. The air smells like rain on old rock. A group of pilgrims moves slowly up the street in silence, led by a Franciscan friar carrying a worn wooden cross. Their feet make small sounds on the wet stone. No one speaks.

At the Fourth Station, a woman stops. She is wearing a gray wool coat. She presses one hand flat against the wall. She closes her eyes.

She stays that way long enough that the group moves on without her.

Whatever she is carrying, she has brought it here. Whatever she is asking, she is asking it in this place, on this stone, against this wall that has held thousands of hands before hers.

This is what the Via Dolorosa does. It receives.

---

## The Turn (connecting Carol to the place)

Carol does not know about the woman in the gray coat. She is in Tennessee, in a kitchen that smells like hospital supply bags and strong coffee, writing three words on a small piece of paper.

"Carry him, Lord."

She folds it. She does not know exactly where it will go. She knows it will go to Jerusalem. She knows someone will place it on the Via Dolorosa - somewhere along the road where Jesus walked carrying something heavier than she can imagine, and still kept walking.

She trusts that is enough.

It is Lent. The season of walking toward something hard because you believe what is on the other side.

She presses a stamp on the envelope.

---

## Emotional Truth

The Via Dolorosa is not a museum. It is not a tourist attraction.
It is the place where God in human form carried suffering up a hill, and the hill did not get smaller.

Believers come to it because their hills are not getting smaller either.

They come because somewhere on that road is the truth that suffering is not the end of the story - but the suffering is real, and it is witnessed, and it is not carried alone.

Carol knows this. She learned it in the book of Isaiah, in the fourteenth verse of the fifty-third chapter.
"He was despised and rejected by mankind, a man of suffering, and familiar with pain."

He is familiar with it.
That is the thing that holds her.
