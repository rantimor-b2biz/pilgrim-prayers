# Voice DNA

> How Pilgrim Prayers sounds — warm, faithful, human, and grounded. Like a trusted companion walking beside you on a sacred journey.

---

## Voice Overview

**In one sentence:** Pilgrim Prayers sounds like a gentle, faith-filled friend who speaks simply and sincerely — offering comfort without pressure, and reverence without religiosity. We speak *to* people, not *at* them.

---

## Voice Attributes

### 1. Faithful but not preachy

**What this means:**
- Rooted in deep faith and reverence for sacred places
- Speaks from a place of genuine belief and care
- Uses everyday language that anyone can understand

**What this doesn't mean:**
- Not lecturing or moralizing
- Not using heavy religious jargon or theological terminology
- Not trying to convert — we serve those who already believe

---

### 2. Warm but not sentimental

**What this means:**
- Calm, reassuring, and compassionate
- Acknowledges the vulnerability of the moment
- Makes people feel seen, heard, and supported

**What this doesn't mean:**
- Not overly emotional or dramatic
- Not sugary or exaggerated
- Not making grand promises about outcomes

---

### 3. Grounded but not cold

**What this means:**
- Clear, honest, and transparent about what we do
- Real people, real locations, real delivery — and we show it
- Trustworthy and straightforward

**What this doesn't mean:**
- Not clinical or detached
- Not transactional or business-like
- Never reduces prayer to a product or service

---

## Language Choices

### Words We Use
- Prayer, faith, blessing, sacred — because that's what this is
- Care, reverence, placed with love — because that's how we do it
- Real, authentic, personally — because proof and trust matter
- Hope, peace, comfort, connection — because that's what people feel

### Words We Avoid
- Marketing hype — "amazing deal," "limited offer," "act now"
- Fear or guilt language — "don't miss your chance," "what if it's too late"
- Miracle promises — "guaranteed healing," "prayers always answered"
- Political language of any kind
- Overly formal religious jargon

### Phrases That Sound Like Us
```
"Your prayer will be placed with care, as if it were our own."

"You don't have to be in Jerusalem to be spiritually present."

"We carry your prayer to a sacred place — so you can feel closer, even from far away."

"Your prayer matters. And it deserves a sacred place."
```

### Phrases That Don't Sound Like Us
```
"Order your prayer placement today!" → Instead: "Submit your prayer"
"Guaranteed spiritual results!" → Instead: We never promise outcomes
"Don't miss this opportunity!" → Instead: "Whenever you're ready, we're here"
"Buy now and save!" → Instead: We don't use commercial pressure
```

---

## Writing Style Rules

### Sentence Length
- Short sentences for emotional impact
- Longer sentences when explaining the process
- Always clear, never complicated

### Paragraph Length
- Short and breathing — space for reflection
- One idea per paragraph
- Let the reader pause

### Formatting
- Highlight key points with warmth, not urgency
- Use lists when clarity helps
- Keep it simple and accessible

### Punctuation
- Periods for calm, grounded statements
- Em dashes for — gentle pauses
- Minimal exclamation marks — we don't shout

## Voice Examples

### Good Example (Social Post)
```
"A prayer was placed at the Western Wall this morning.

It was written by a mother in Ohio — for her son, for his health, for his future.

She couldn't be there. But her prayer was.

That's what Pilgrim Prayers is about."

Why it works:
- Simple, human, and grounding
- Shows the real experience without being preachy
- Ends with a quiet statement, not a call to action
```

### Bad Example (Social Post)
```
"Don't miss your chance to have your prayers placed at the HOLIEST sites in Jerusalem!
Order now and receive a special blessing package! Limited spots available!"

What's wrong:
- Marketing hype and urgency
- Treats prayer as a product
- Uses pressure instead of comfort
```

---

## The "Sound Like Us" Test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Would a believer feel comforted reading this?
2. Does it sound like a real person who cares, not a company selling something?
3. Is it free of pressure, hype, or guilt?
4. Are we promising only what we can deliver — care, reverence, and real placement?
5. Would someone skeptical of online services feel their concerns were respected?
