# Writing Rules for Weekly Posts

## Post Basics

**What works for Pilgrim Prayers:**
- A single story, place, or moment — deeply told
- Real details that make the reader feel present
- Faith expressed through experience, not theology
- A gentle invitation at the end — never a hard sell

**Post length:** 500-800 words
- Under 500: Too thin — doesn't build enough emotion
- Over 800: Too long for weekly rhythm — save it for special occasions
- Sweet spot: 600-700 words

---

## The Opening (First 2-3 Sentences)

The opening must place the reader somewhere. They should see, hear, or feel something before they read anything "about" Pilgrim Prayers.

### Opening Formula 1: Place First
```
"The Garden of Gethsemane is quietest at dawn. The olive trees — some over 2,000 years old — stand in the same silence that surrounded Jesus on his final night."
```

### Opening Formula 2: Person First
```
"She wrote three words on a small piece of paper. She folded it carefully, as if it carried everything she couldn't say out loud."
```

### Opening Formula 3: Moment First
```
"There is a moment, just before sunrise, when the Western Wall turns gold. The stones seem to warm before the air does."
```

### Opening Formula 4: Scripture First
```
"'I lift up my eyes to the mountains — where does my help come from?' (Psalm 121:1). Standing on the Mount of Olives, those words become more than poetry."
```

### Opening Formula 5: Seasonal First
```
"Spring arrives quietly in Jerusalem. The wildflowers appear first — scattered across the hillsides of the Kidron Valley like small, bright prayers."
```

### Opening Formula 6: Question First
```
"What happens to a prayer after it's placed at the Western Wall? The paper may weather. The ink may fade. But the prayer — the prayer endures."
```

## Core Writing Rules

### Rule 1: One Place, One Story, One Feeling
Don't try to cover everything. One location, one narrative, one emotional takeaway.

### Rule 2: Show, Don't Tell
```
Don't tell: "Jerusalem is a spiritual place"
Show: "The afternoon call to prayer echoes across the Old City as church bells begin to ring — and at the Western Wall, a woman presses her forehead against the stone and whispers."
```

### Rule 3: Scripture as Story, Not Sermon
```
✅ "Jesus knelt here, in this very garden, and prayed, 'Not my will, but yours be done' (Luke 22:42). The olive trees around him that night may still be standing."
❌ "Luke 22:42 teaches us that we must submit to God's will in all circumstances."
```

### Rule 4: The Invitation is Always Gentle
```
✅ "If there's a prayer on your heart today, it has a place in Jerusalem."
✅ "Whenever you're ready, we're here to carry your prayer."
❌ "Submit your prayer today!"
❌ "Don't wait — place your prayer now!"
```

### Rule 5: End with Warmth, Not a Pitch
The last line should leave the reader with a feeling — not a sales message.

```
✅ "Your prayer matters. And it deserves a sacred place."
✅ "Jerusalem is closer than you think."
❌ "Click here to order your prayer placement."
```
